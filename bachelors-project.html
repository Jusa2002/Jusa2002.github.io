Bachelor's Final Project

Impact of Seasonality on Predator-Prey Dynamics

Overview

The Lotka-Volterra equations are one of the most well-known mathematical models in ecology. They describe the cyclical dynamics between a prey population and a predator population through a pair of coupled differential equations: prey grow when predators are scarce, predators thrive when prey are abundant, and the two populations oscillate in a feedback loop. Despite its simplicity, this model serves as a foundation for more complex ecological modelling.

The focus of this project was to extend the standard Lotka-Volterra system into a non-autonomous model — one where the parameters are no longer constant but vary periodically with time, simulating the effect of seasons. In practice, prey reproduction rates peak in spring and summer, while predators may reduce their hunting activity in winter. By making parameters like birth rate and predation rate periodic functions of time (with period T = 1 year), the model becomes significantly more complex and realistic.

The analysis focused on the qualitative behaviour of solutions: under what conditions do populations converge to a limit cycle? How sensitive are the dynamics to initial conditions? To answer these questions, the project made use of Poincaré maps — a classical tool from dynamical systems theory that reduces the study of periodic orbits to finding fixed points of a discrete map. Numerical simulations were carried out in MATLAB using ode45, and the report also proposed and analysed an original variant of the seasonal model.
